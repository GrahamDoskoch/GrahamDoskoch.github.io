About Me

I've been a PhD student in astronomy at West Virginia University since the fall of 2020. I do research on pulsars and pulsar timing, one of the main methods used for detecting gravitational waves. In a pulsar timing array, astronomers monitor dozens of millisecond pulsars, whose signals are expected to arrive on Earth at precise intervals. Low-frequency gravitational waves can change these times of arrival, and so gravitational wave sources show up as shifts in times of arrival across the entire array. While LIGO looks for gravitational waves at frequencies of tens or hundreds of hertz, pulsar timing arrays are sensitive to sources at nanohertz frequencies, like supermassive black hole binaries and cosmic strings. As of 2020, no pulsar timing array has made a confirmed detection, but we hope to change that over the next decade. You can learn more about the goals and principles behind pulsar timing from the [North American Nanohertz Observatory for Gravitational Waves](http://nanograv.org/research/), or NANOGRAV. At Swarthmore, I worked on x-ray spectroscopy of the winds of massive O and B stars using data from the Chandra X-ray Observatory. A paper on our group's results has been submitted and is undergoing peer review.
