About Me

I'm Graham, a second-year PhD student in the Department of Physics and Astronomy at West Virginia University. My work is centered on pulsars, fast-spinning neutron stars emitting beams of radio waves, and gravitational wave detection via pulsar timing arrays.

I graduated from Swarthmore College in 2020 with a degree in astrophysics and mathematics. While I was there, I worked with David Cohen to use x-ray spectra from Chandra to study the winds of massive O and B stars. Pulsars have always been what I'm most passionate about, which is why I ended up in WVU, working with Prof. Maura McLaughlin at the Center for Gravitational Waves and Cosmology.

I also enjoy science communication, and outreach is a part of my academic life. I write for Astrobites and also mentor high school students interested in astronomy. When I'm not coding or writing, you can find me doing crosswords or hiking through the beautiful hills of West Virginia.

Research

On the whole, I'm a pulsar person. Pulsars are fast-spinning neutron stars with powerful magnetic fields that give rise to beams of light - often radio waves - emanating from their magnetic poles. Since a pulsar's magnetic and rotational axes are usually misaligned, this means that these beams sweep across the sky, like a cosmic lighthouse. From our Earth-bound point of view, they appear to be brief, periodic pulses, often with beautiful, complex structure.

There are plenty of subfields in pulsar astronomy; my work mostly falls into a couple particular categories.

Pulsar timing arrays and gravitational wave detection
When someone talks about gravitational waves, the first thing that comes to mind might be ground-based interferometers like LIGO, Virgo and KAGRA, which brought gravitational waves into the public eye with their first direct detections in 2014. These instruments can only study a small portion of the gravitational wave spectrum. Imagine if we could only see the universe through optical telescopes. We'd miss out on so much.

Interferometers like LIGO operate very roughly in the 10 hertz to 10 kilohertz frequency band of the gravitational wave spectrum, limiting what sorts of sources they can observe to things like merging stellar-mass or intermediate-mass binary black holes, or gravitational waves from supernovae. However, binary supermassive black holes also emit gravitational waves, with frequencies in the nanohertz range, forming a stochastic gravitational wave background.

Pulsar timing arrays are designed to detect binary supermassive black holes and other low-frequency sources by observing numerous pulsars with spin periods of around a few milliseconds. Passing gravitational waves should change when pulses arrive at our telescopes, and so by looking for some characteristic behavior in our pulsars, we can try and detect this stochastic background and other interesting things.

I'm an associate member of the North American Nanohertz Observatory for Gravitational Waves, or NANOGrav. We've come very close to detecting the stochastic background and have already seen hints of it. My PhD thesis will be on expanding our results to become part of the International Pulsar Timing Array (IPTA). The IPTA is a collection of pulsar timing arrays from around the world, aiming to create the most sensitive pulsar timing dataset ever. My role is to work on pipelines to make it easier to add new telescopes and datasets into the mix, allowing the IPTA to keep expanding and becoming more and more sensitive.

Rotating radio transients and single-pulse timing
Not all pulsars are so regular! Some undergo unusual phenomena light nulling and mode-changing, in which their emission can change or even turn off for a time. A subpopulation of pulsars give rise to emission so sporadic that they have their own name: rotating radio transients, or RRATs (pronounced "rats" or "r-rats" - your choice!). They're still not well-understood, although plenty of mechanisms have been proposed over the last decade to explain them, including radiation belts, asteroids and the fallback of supernova material. They also might simply be old pulsars whose emission mechanisms are failing.

Currently, I'm studying a group of RRATs discovered by the PALFA survey at the Arecibo Observatory. PALFA was a survey running from 2004 to 2020, searching at L-band using Arecibo's ALFA receiver. It's yielded numerous millisecond pulsars and RRATs, and the dozen or so RRATs I'm working on are some of the few that have yet to be analyzed.

Single-pulse analyses can be useful in other realms of pulsar science. Some pulsars, like the Crab Pulsar, often emit giant pulses, much more energetic than normal ones. While the Crab isn't a RRAT, analyzing giant pulses using single-pulse techniques can give us clues as to what's behind them, possibly allowing glimpses into pulsar emission mechanisms. I'm currently co-leading a project for the Pulsar Search Collaboratory studying giant pulses from the Crab using the 20-meter telescope at the Green Bank Observatory.

Outreach

Astrobites
I write for Astrobites, a group of graduate students covering the latest research in astronomy and supported by the American Astronomical Society. We aim to write about preprints on arXiv at a level understandable by undergraduates. I like to write about the same topics I study - pulsars, gravitational wave detection, and radio astronomy in general. You can read my latest pieces here.

Pulsar Search Collaboratory
I'm a mentor for the Pulsar Search Collaboratory, a collaboration between WVU and the Green Bank Observatory, conducting outreach and teaching students how to search for and study pulsars. High schoolers and undergraduates get the chance to learn about working being done at Green Bank, search through data for candidate pulsars, and perform their own observations and participate in research projects.

Pulsar of the Day
Astronomers have discovered over 3000 pulsars over the past half century, and each and every one of them is interesting in its own way. I run the @PulsarOfTheDay Twitter account, which picks a random pulsar each day and tells you about its properties.
